Billion Dollar Extreme Weather Events in the U.S. Increasing Due To Climate Change

Time to sound the alarm bells: climate change has a devastating impact on the severity and frequency of extreme weather. Now, extreme weather events are costing taxpayers more than ever before.

By Emily Sternlicht

Oct. 13, 2021

Credit: Flood Research & Action Center

In 2020, extreme weather cost taxpayers $98.9 billion. These costs have been increasing steadily since 1980, starting at $18.4 billion, when the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) began tracking these events — and it’s getting worse.

18 billion-dollar extreme weather events occured from January-September 2021, costing upwards of $100 billion in losses due to property and infrastructure damage, already exeeding costs from last year.

In August 2021, the U.N. Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change released a report that attributes these events to climate change. It's clear that the U.S. will likely experience more of these events well into the future.

Here are the three most expensive extreme weather events in the United States:

Hurricanes

Hurricanes and tropical cyclones account for the highest costing extreme weather events. Since 1980 they cost $1.03 trillion, with an average of $19.9 billion per event. Ranking in the top five most costly hurricanes on record Hurricane Ida is the most expensive disaster this year, so far exceeding $60 billion, and its total cost is yet to be determined.

Floods and rain

Flooding and flash floods caused by rain cost $151 billion since 1980 and are the third-most frequent billion-dollar extreme weather event that the U.S. experiences. In Sept. 2021, New York City saw 3.15 inches of rain fall in one hour, surpassing the record breaking 1.94 inches just a few weeks before, costing up to $100 million in damages to the MTA alone.

Wildfires

Every year the Western U.S. is ravaged by wildfires, and studies show that wildfires are only going to get more intense and destructive. 2018 was the most expensive year, costing $25.7 billion. In the summer of 2021 California has already spent $610 million to fight the Dixie Fire, the most expensive in the state's history.
